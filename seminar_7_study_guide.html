Economic Development

The Great Convergence: Development Success

Between 1990 and 2015, the world witnessed unprecedented improvements in human development:

  • Poverty reduction: The extreme poverty rate plummeted from 37% to just 10%â€”a 73% decline that lifted 1.2 billion people out of destitution.
  • Child survival: Under-5 mortality rates fell dramatically, from 93 deaths per 1,000 births to 43 per 1,000â€”representing millions of young lives saved.
  • Longevity gains: Life expectancy in developing countries increased by 8 years, from 62 to 70 years, reflecting better healthcare, nutrition, and living conditions.
  • Educational progress: Youth literacy rates (ages 15-24) rose from 82% to 91%, expanding opportunities for economic participation and social mobility.

This "Great Convergence" demonstrates how economic development, technological progress, and improved governance can rapidly transform human welfare on a global scale.

4. Globalization & Trade

  • Global value chains integrate developing economies

5. Science & Technology

  • Mobile phones: 4 billion subscriptions enable financial inclusion
  • Green Revolution: agricultural productivity soars
  • Medical advances: vaccines, antibiotics, oral rehydration therapy
  • Knowledge transfer: internet democratizes access to information

Development Economics: Theoretical Perspectives

Modernization Theory (Rostow): Linear stages from traditional to modern economy

  • Evidence: East Asian Tigers followed similar trajectory
  • Critique: Ignores historical context, path dependencies

Dependency Theory (Frank, Wallerstein): Core exploits periphery through unequal exchange

  • Evidence: Colonial extraction, terms of trade deterioration
  • Critique: Can't explain East Asian success, China/India rise

Institutional Economics (North, Acemoglu): Inclusive institutions drive growth

  • Evidence: Property rights, rule of law correlate with prosperity
  • Critique: Endogeneity problem (do good institutions cause growth or vice versa?)

Pinker's Synthesis: Multiple factors interactâ€”markets + institutions + technology + peace + knowledge

The Inequality Debates

Central Controversy: Rising Inequality as Crisis?

Conventional wisdom (2010s): Inequality rising catastrophically; only rich benefit from growth; middle class hollowed out

Pinker's contrarian claim: Inequality rising in some dimensions, falling in others; absolute living standards improving for all

Conceptual Distinctions

1. Inequality â‰  Poverty

  • Igor and Boris parable: If Boris's goat dies, inequality decreases but neither is better off
  • Frankfurt's argument: What matters morally is "enough," not relative position
  • Implication: Focus on absolute living standards, not Gini coefficients

2. Types of Inequality

  • International inequality: Between countries (declining)
  • Global inequality: Among all world citizens (declining)
  • Within-country inequality: Within nations like US, UK (rising since 1980)
  • Income inequality: Market income distribution (rising)
  • Consumption inequality: What people actually consume (flat/declining)

3. Anonymous vs. Longitudinal Data

  • Anonymous: Statistical quintiles at different time points
  • Longitudinal: Tracking actual individuals over time
  • Key finding: High income mobility means "bottom 20%" consists of different people

The Elephant Curve: Globalization's Winners and Losers

MilanoviÄ‡'s growth incidence curve (1988-2008):

  • Winners (body of elephant): Asian middle class (40-80th percentile globally) +40-60% income gain
  • Winners (trunk tip): Global top 1% +60% income gain
  • Relative losers (trunk dip): Lower-middle class in rich countries (75-85th percentile globally) +10% gain

IPE Interpretation: Globalization creates winners and losers, but 70% of humanity wins; losers still gain (just less)

  • Population-weighted: fell from .63 (1988) to .53 (2011)

Absolute poverty collapsing:

  • Extreme poverty: 90% (1820) â†’ 10% (2015)
  • US poverty (consumption-based): 30% (1960) â†’ 3% (2013)

Living standards rising across distribution:

  • US bottom quintile disposable income: +49% (1979-2010)
  • Poor Americans have air conditioning (80%), cars (60%), dishwashers (40%)
  • Dematerialization: smartphones provide services once costing thousands

Social spending increasing:

  • OECD social transfers: 1.5% GDP (1880-1930) â†’ 22% GDP (2016)
  • US including private mandates: 2nd highest social spending (after France)

Theoretical Debate: What Explains Rising Within-Country Inequality?

Technology hypothesis (SBTC - Skill-Biased Technological Change):

  • Automation replaces routine jobs
  • Premium for high-skill workers increases
  • Winner-take-all dynamics in digital economy

Globalization hypothesis:

  • Offshoring lowers demand for domestic manufacturing labor
  • Trade competition suppresses wages
  • Capital mobility weakens labor bargaining power

Institutional hypothesis:

  • Decline of unions reduces wage compression
  • Financialization increases capital share
  • Tax policy shifts (Reagan/Thatcher reforms)
  • Changes in norms regarding executive compensation

Pinker's view: Multiple factors; inequality rise doesn't negate absolute gains; solutions possible without reversing globalization/technology

Policy Responses to Inequality

  • Progressive taxation: Higher rates on top earners, wealth taxes
  • Education investment: Skills training, STEM education, lifelong learning
  • Social insurance expansion: Universal basic income, negative income tax
  • Labor market policies: Minimum wage increases, collective bargaining rights
  • Competition policy: Antitrust enforcement, platform regulation
  • International coordination: Tax haven crackdowns, global minimum tax

Pinker's preference: Targeted interventions that preserve market incentives while ensuring broad-based prosperity

Global Institutions & Governance

The Liberal International Order

Post-WWII Architecture

  • United Nations (1945): Collective security, international law
  • Bretton Woods System: IMF, World Bank, stable exchange rates
  • GATT/WTO (1947/1995): Trade liberalization, dispute resolution
  • NATO (1949): Military alliance, democratic peace
  • European integration: ECSC â†’ EEC â†’ EU, peace through commerce

Key Functions

  • Coordination: Solve collective action problems
  • Information: Transparency, monitoring, verification
  • Enforcement: Sanctions, reputation, conditionality
  • Legitimacy: Democratic input, rule of law

Evidence of Institutional Effectiveness

The liberal international order has demonstrated remarkable success in fostering cooperation and prosperity:

  • Universal membership: The United Nations has grown from 51 founding members in 1945 to 193 today, reflecting near-universal acceptance of multilateral cooperation principles.
  • Trade integration: World Trade Organization membership expanded from 123 countries at its founding in 1995 to 164 members, facilitating $13 trillion in annual global trade.
  • Dispute resolution: The WTO's binding arbitration system achieves an 80% compliance rate, preventing trade wars and maintaining economic stability without world government.
  • Economic integration: Deep trade relationships create interdependencies that raise the costs of conflict and incentivize peaceful resolution of disputes.

While these institutions face contemporary challenges, their track record demonstrates that international cooperation can work when properly designed and implemented.

Case Study: WTO Dispute Settlement

Problem: How to enforce trade rules without world government?

Solution: Binding arbitration with retaliation rights

Process:

  1. Country files complaint with WTO panel
  2. Panel issues ruling based on WTO law
  3. Appeals possible to Appellate Body
  4. If defendant doesn't comply, complainant authorized to retaliate

Success: 600+ cases filed, 80% compliance rate, prevented trade wars

Crisis: US blocked Appellate Body appointments (2017-2020), system paralyzed

Contemporary Challenges to Global Governance

  • Rise of China: Challenges to Western-dominated institutions
  • Populist nationalism: Brexit, Trump's "America First," anti-globalization
  • Institutional gridlock: UN Security Council vetoes, WTO reform paralysis
  • New issues: Cyber governance, climate change, pandemic response
  • Legitimacy deficits: Democratic accountability, elite capture

Pinker's view: Institutions imperfect but preferable to anarchy; reform rather than abandon

Trade & Globalization

The Economic Logic of Trade

Comparative Advantage (Ricardo, 1817)

  • Core insight: Countries benefit from trade even if one is better at everything
  • Opportunity cost: Focus on what you're relatively best at
  • Mutual gains: Trade expands consumption possibilities for both parties
  • Empirical support: Countries with higher trade-to-GDP ratios are richer

Modern Trade Theory

  • Economies of scale: Larger markets enable specialization
  • Product differentiation: Intra-industry trade in varieties
  • Global value chains: Tasks dispersed across countries
  • Technology transfer: Diffusion of ideas and best practices

Empirical Evidence for Trade Benefits

Historical evidence:

  • First wave of globalization (1870-1914): rapid growth, living standards rise
  • Interwar deglobalization (1914-1945): depression, autarky, conflict
  • Post-war re-globalization (1945+): unprecedented prosperity

Cross-country studies:

  • Trade openness correlates with GDP per capita (correlation â‰ˆ 0.6)
  • Countries that liberalized trade grew faster (Sachs & Warner, 1995)
  • Export-led growth strategies successful in East Asia

Natural experiments:

  • East/West Germany: market integration vs. autarky
  • China pre/post-WTO: trade explosion coincides with growth acceleration
  • NAFTA: Mexico's manufacturing exports soar

The Globalization Backlash

Critiques of globalization:

  • Job displacement: Manufacturing decline in rich countries
  • Wage stagnation: Competition from low-wage countries
  • Race to the bottom: Environmental and labor standards
  • Cultural homogenization: McDonaldization, loss of local traditions
  • Democratic deficit: Corporate power, regulatory capture

Pinker's response:

  • Trade creates more jobs than it destroys (creative destruction)
  • Consumers benefit from lower prices, greater variety
  • Environmental and labor standards can be maintained through regulation
  • Cultural exchange enriches rather than impoverishes societies
  • Institutions can be reformed to increase democratic accountability

China Shock: Lessons for Trade Policy

Autor, Dorn, and Hanson findings:

  • Chinese imports caused 1 million US manufacturing job losses (2000-2007)
  • Local communities slow to adjust; persistent unemployment
  • Political consequences: support for protectionist politicians

Policy implications:

  • Trade Adjustment Assistance: Retraining, relocation aid
  • Place-based policies: Regional development programs
  • Progressive taxation: Compensate trade's losers
  • Gradual integration: Slower liberalization schedules

Pinker's view: Address adjustment costs, don't abandon trade's benefits

Environmental Economics

The Environmental Kuznets Curve

Hypothesis

  • Early development: Environmental quality deteriorates as income rises
  • Turning point: Around $8,000-$15,000 per capita
  • Later development: Environmental quality improves with higher income
  • Mechanisms: Cleaner technology, service economy, environmental regulation

Evidence

  • Air pollution: SO2, particulates decline with income in rich countries
  • Water quality: Rivers cleaner in developed nations
  • Forest cover: Reforestation in Europe, North America
  • Species protection: Endangered species acts, conservation efforts

Environmental Progress Evidence

Pinker presents compelling evidence that environmental quality has dramatically improved in developed nations:

  • Air pollution reduction: US sulfur dioxide emissions fell by 88% from their 1973 peak, while carbon monoxide emissions dropped 59% since 1970. London's air quality has improved by over 90% since the deadly Great Smog of 1952.
  • Forest recovery: US forest cover has increased by 43% since its low point in 1920, now covering 33% of the land area.
  • Water quality: Rivers and lakes in developed countries are significantly cleaner than they were in the mid-20th century.
  • Conservation success: Endangered species protection programs have prevented numerous extinctions and enabled population recoveries.

This demonstrates the Environmental Kuznets Curve in action: as societies become wealthier, they invest more in environmental protection and cleaner technologies.

Democracy & Peace

The Democratic Peace Theory

Core Claims

  • Dyadic version: Democracies rarely fight wars against each other
  • Monadic version: Democracies are generally less war-prone
  • Institutional explanation: Checks and balances, public accountability
  • Normative explanation: Democratic values of compromise, negotiation

Evidence

  • No clear case of established democracies fighting each other since 1945
  • Democratic dyads have lower conflict propensity (Russett & Oneal)
  • Transition periods may be more conflict-prone (Mansfield & Snyder)

The Long Peace: Statistical Evidence

Pinker documents a remarkable decline in violent conflict since World War II:

  • Great power peace: No wars between major powers for over 75 years since 1945â€”the longest such period in modern history.
  • Declining lethality: Battle death rates fell by 92% from the 1950s to the 2000s, even accounting for population growth.
  • Fewer civil wars: The number of ongoing civil wars dropped from 12 in 1990 to just 4 by 2016, contributing to global stability.
  • Democratic expansion: The number of democracies worldwide reached 116 by 2018, supporting theories about democratic peace and institutional conflict resolution.

This "Long Peace" reflects the combined effects of nuclear deterrence, economic interdependence, international institutions, and the spread of democratic normsâ€”all consistent with liberal international relations theory.

Human Rights

The Rights Revolution

Historical Expansion

Human Rights Progress Evidence

The expansion of human rights over the past two centuries represents one of humanity's greatest moral achievements:

  • Abolition of slavery: Once a universal practice across civilizations, slavery is now illegal in every country on Earth, though enforcement challenges remain.
  • Women's political participation: In 1900, no country granted women full political rights. By 2015, all 193 UN member states had extended voting rights to women, representing a complete transformation of political inclusion.
  • Religious freedom: Religious tolerance, rare throughout most of history, is now protected in most national constitutions, though implementation varies and setbacks occur in some regions.
  • LGBTQ+ rights: Same-sex marriage was illegal everywhere as recently as 2000, but by 2020 had become legal in 29 countries, demonstrating rapid social change.

This progress reflects the expansion of the "moral circle"â€”our growing recognition that all humans deserve equal dignity and rights.

Existential Challenges

Why Existential Risks Matter for IPE

Definition: Risks that could cause human extinction or permanent civilizational collapse

Categories:

  • Anthropogenic risks: Nuclear war, climate change, AI alignment

Current Status

  • Nuclear states: 9 countries possess nuclear weapons

Climate Change: Economic Analysis

Economic research demonstrates that climate action is cost-effective compared to inaction:

  • Cost of inaction: The Stern Review estimates that uncontrolled global warming could permanently reduce global GDP by 5-20%, with the poorest countries suffering disproportionately.
  • Mitigation costs: Preventing dangerous climate change would cost approximately 1% of global GDP annuallyâ€”a manageable investment for most developed economies.
  • Economic returns: Climate investments offer 5-20:1 returns, making mitigation one of the highest-return policy interventions available.
  • Contemporary estimates: The EPA values the social cost of carbon at $51-185 per ton, reflecting both direct damages and systemic financial risks from climate change.

This analysis supports Pinker's argument that environmental protection and economic growth can be compatible with proper pricing of externalities and technological innovation.
