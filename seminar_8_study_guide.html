Central IR Questions Addressed

  1. State Sovereignty vs. Human Rights: How do states balance border control with humanitarian obligations?
  2. Security: What constitutes security when threats are economic, social, and environmental rather than military?
  3. Cooperation: Why does international cooperation on migration remain so difficult?

  5. Non-State Actors: What roles do cartels, NGOs, and transnational networks play?

Key Empirical Evidence

Nazario's investigative journalism reveals critical data about Central American migration:

  • Scale of child migration: An estimated 48,000+ children entered the US alone and illegally in 2000; this surged to 100,000 annually by 2011.
  • Journey dangers: Every other day in Chiapas, Mexico, a migrant loses a limb to freight trains; 300+ migrants die annually crossing the US-Mexico border.
  • Sexual violence: Nearly 1 in 6 migrant girls detained in Texas report sexual assault during their journey, according to a University of Houston study.
  • Economic drivers: Remittances from migrants contribute over $40 billion annually to Latin American economies, making migration an economic necessity for many families.
"This is the adventure story of the twenty-first century. Children who set out on this journey usually don't make it all the way there. They end up back in Central America, defeated."

Migration Theory & Frameworks

Push-Pull Model in Action

Push Factors (Honduras/Central America)

  • Economic deprivation: 90% of Hondurans earn $1/day or less; factory workers make $40-$120/month
  • Limited opportunities: 11% of children never attend school; jobs scarce for those without connections
  • Family separation: Parents already in US create family reunification imperative
  • Violence: Honduras has world's highest murder rate; gang recruitment threatens youth
  • Environmental factors: Limited arable land, natural disasters, climate stress

Pull Factors (United States)

  • Economic opportunity: Wages 10-50x higher than in Central America
  • Family networks: 82% of LA nannies, 25% of housecleaners are mothers with children in home country
  • Demand for labor: Agriculture, construction, domestic work, food service
  • Social capital: Established immigrant communities provide support
  • Aspirational: Images of US prosperity seen on TV create powerful draw

Theoretical Perspectives on Migration

Neoclassical Economics (Micro):

  • Individuals are rational actors maximizing expected income
  • Evidence: Lourdes calculates she can earn 10-20x more in US
  • Critique: Ignores non-economic factors (family bonds, trauma)

New Economics of Migration (Household):

  • Families make collective decisions to diversify risk
  • Evidence: Lourdes migrates so children can stay in school, avoid poverty
  • Critique: Underestimates psychological costs of separation

Dual Labor Market Theory (Structural):

  • Developed economies have structural demand for immigrant labor
  • Evidence: US employers want "accommodating, low-cost workers" for 3-D jobs
  • Critique: Why then the enforcement theater?

World Systems Theory (Marxist):

  • Migration flows from periphery to core driven by capitalist exploitation
  • Evidence: Historical US intervention in Central America creates conditions driving migration
  • Critique: Migrants exercise agency; not simply exploited victims

Social Network Theory:

  • Migration perpetuates through network effects; costs/risks decline
  • Evidence: Enrique follows path others have taken; smuggler networks facilitate
  • Supports: Migration as self-sustaining phenomenon

Case Study: The Feminization of Migration

Context: Since 1990s, increasing numbers of single mothers migrate from Central America

Drivers:

  • Divorce/separation leaves women without income from fathers
  • Gendered labor markets create demand for domestic workers in US
  • Male migration opened pathway for women to follow

Consequences:

  • USC study: 82% of live-in LA nannies have children in home country
  • Children left behind experience trauma, abandonment, anger
  • "Transnational motherhood" challenges traditional family structures
  • Second generation follows: Children like Enrique seeking mothers

IR Significance: Gendered dimensions of migration reshape social structures in both sending and receiving countries

The Migration Journey: Quantitative Reality

Enrique's statistics:

  • 7 failed attempts before success on 8th try
  • 122 days from final departure to reunion
  • Rode atop 30+ freight trains
  • Lost 28 pounds during journey
  • Cost family $1,700 in smuggling fees

Broader patterns:

  • Average of 8 attempts for successful border crossing (2000)
  • 50% of Central Americans entering illegally use smugglers
  • Smuggling fees: $1,200-$10,000 depending on route, method
  • Mexico deports 200,000+ Central Americans annually (peak years)

Debate: Is Migration "Voluntary"?

Traditional view: Economic migrants choose to move; refugees are forced

Book's evidence:

  • Lourdes: "I left for them" (economic necessity, not choice)
  • Enrique: Pulled by family bonds, pushed by drugs/poverty
  • María Isabel: Chooses to leave daughter, replicating cycle

IR Implication: Binary forced/voluntary distinction breaks down; most migration involves "constrained choice" due to structural factors

Human Security

From State Security to Human Security

Traditional Security Paradigm

  • Referent object: The state
  • Threats: Military aggression, territorial violation
  • Solutions: Defense spending, alliances, deterrence
  • Actors: States, militaries

Human Security Paradigm (UNDP 1994)

  • Referent object: Individual people
  • Threats: "Freedom from fear" + "freedom from want"
  • Dimensions: Economic, food, health, environmental, personal, community, political security
  • Solutions: Development, governance, human rights

Human Insecurity in Enrique's Journey

Economic insecurity (Honduras):

  • Lourdes cannot afford to feed children; they go to bed hungry
  • Enrique at age 10 must work selling spices to survive
  • 30% of Honduran children ages 5 and under are stunted from malnutrition

Food insecurity:

  • "Sleep facedown so your stomach won't growl so much"—Lourdes to children
  • School principal reports students too malnourished to stand for anthem
  • Children pulled from school at age 8 to work or babysit

Health insecurity:

  • Limited access to doctors, medicine
  • Migrants drink contaminated water, contract diseases
  • Injuries from trains: limb loss, head trauma, untreated infections

Personal security:

  • Gang violence: Mara Salvatrucha, 18th Street control neighborhoods
  • Police violence: Beatings, robbery, rape of migrants
  • Zeta cartel: Kidnapping, torture, murder of migrants in Mexico

Community/family security:

  • Family separation creates profound psychological trauma
  • Children left behind lack parental guidance, supervision
  • Social fabric torn by out-migration

Case Study: The Train Tops—"La Bestia"

Context: Central Americans must cross Mexico atop freight trains

Security threats documented:

  • Physical danger: Every other day a migrant loses limb in Chiapas; derailments, falls, electrocution
  • Criminal violence: Gangsters rob, beat, throw migrants off moving trains; Enrique beaten nearly to death
  • Sexual violence: Systematic rape of women and girls; some men assaulted too
  • State violence: Police rob migrants, beat them, demand bribes; immigration agents torture detainees
  • Environmental: Extreme heat/cold, starvation, dehydration, disease

Survival rate: Estimated only 50% of those who attempt journey actually reach destination

IR Significance: Demonstrates how human insecurity creates humanitarian crisis requiring international response

Critical Debate: Security for Whom?

State perspective: Unauthorized migration threatens:

  • Territorial sovereignty and rule of law
  • Economic interests of native workers
  • Public services capacity (schools, hospitals)
  • National identity and social cohesion

Migrant perspective: Security requires:

  • Ability to provide for family (economic security)
  • Family unity (community security)
  • Protection from violence (personal security)
  • Access to basic needs (food, health security)

IR Question: When state security and human security conflict, which should take priority? Can they be reconciled?

State Sovereignty & Border Control

The Sovereignty Paradox

Classical Westphalian Sovereignty

  • Territorial integrity: State controls its borders
  • Authority: State determines who can enter/exit
  • Non-interference: Other states respect immigration decisions
  • Citizenship: State defines membership, rights

Contemporary Challenges

  • Enforcement limits: Despite massive spending, borders remain porous
  • Economic interdependence: Economies depend on immigrant labor
  • Human rights norms: International obligations limit state discretion
  • Transnational networks: Migration sustained by forces beyond state control

Border Enforcement Reality: The US-Mexico Case

Enforcement expansion:

  • Border Patrol agents: 5x increase since 1993 (4,000 → 21,000+)
  • Border spending: $11.9 billion annually (2010)
  • Physical barriers: 651 miles of wall/fencing constructed
  • Technology: Sensors, drones, cameras, biometric systems

Results (according to PPIC study):

  • No evidence strategy worked to deter unauthorized entry
  • Illegal immigrant population grew faster after buildup began
  • Deaths increased: 300+ annually from more dangerous crossing routes
  • Smuggler use rose: 70% → 90% of migrants employ coyotes
  • Settlement pattern changed: Migrants stay rather than circulate

IR Implication: Unilateral border enforcement has limited effectiveness; creates unintended consequences

Case Study: Mexico's Dual Role

Context: Mexico is both transit country for Central Americans and sending country to US

Mexico's sovereignty challenges:

  • Southern border: Porous 600-mile border with Guatemala; limited enforcement capacity
  • Interior: Weak state presence in rural areas; corruption endemic
  • Cartel control: Zetas, Gulf Cartel control territory, extort migrants
  • Law enforcement: Police rob migrants rather than protect them

US pressure on Mexico:

  • US demands Mexico enforce immigration law against Central Americans
  • Mexico deports 200,000+ Central Americans annually to appease US
  • Yet Mexico criticizes US treatment of Mexican migrants
  • Hypocrisy: Mexico demands rights for its citizens abroad but denies them to Central Americans in Mexico

Sovereignty implications:

  • Externalization of US border to Mexico's southern border
  • US effectively dictating Mexico's immigration policy
  • Demonstrates how powerful states can undermine weaker states' sovereignty

The Schizophrenic Policy Debate

Thesis: US pursues contradictory immigration policy

Front door (enforcement):

  • Massive border security apparatus
  • Workplace raids, deportations (400,000/year at peak)
  • State-level anti-immigrant legislation
  • Political rhetoric about "securing borders"

Back door (tolerance):

  • Minimal workplace enforcement until recently
  • No mandatory E-Verify system
  • Industries dependent on unauthorized workers (agriculture, construction, services)
  • Economic benefits: cheap labor, consumer prices, economic growth

Why the contradiction?

  • Business interests want labor; voters want enforcement
  • Politicians talk tough but don't want economic disruption
  • Result: "enforcement theater" that satisfies neither objective

Sovereignty vs. Human Rights: Legal Framework

State sovereignty claims:

  • Right to exclude non-citizens
  • Right to deport those illegally present
  • Right to prioritize citizens' interests

Human rights obligations:

  • Non-refoulement (can't return to danger)
  • Family unity rights (ICCPR, CRC)
  • Children's best interests (Convention on Rights of Child)
  • Due process protections

Tensions revealed in book:

  • US detains minors in prison-like conditions
  • Mexico deports children without protection screening
  • Family separation policies violate human rights norms
  • Yet states claim sovereign right to enforce immigration law

Non-State Actors

The Complex Actor Landscape

Enrique's journey reveals migration is shaped by diverse actors beyond states:

Criminal Networks

  • Smugglers (coyotes): Profit-driven facilitators charging $1,200-$10,000
  • Gangs (MS-13, 18th Street): Rob, assault, kill migrants on train routes
  • Drug cartels (Zetas): Kidnapping operations generating millions
  • Corrupt officials: Police, immigration agents extracting bribes

Humanitarian Actors

  • Religious organizations: Churches providing food, shelter, spiritual support
  • NGOs: Human rights groups documenting abuses, advocacy
  • Grassroots networks: "Food throwers," local residents offering aid
  • Medical workers: Treating injured migrants for free/low cost

Economic Actors

  • Employers: US businesses hiring unauthorized workers
  • Remittance companies: Western Union, MoneyGram facilitating transfers
  • Transportation: Railroad companies, bus lines, truck drivers

Case Study: Padre Leo and the Church Network

Actor: Father Leonardo López Guajardo, Parroquia de San José, Nuevo Laredo

Activities:

  • Provides daily meals to migrants (hundreds served)
  • Distributes food cards valid for 15 days
  • Offers shelter for women migrants in his own apartment
  • Arranges medical care, haircuts, donated clothing
  • Helps migrants receive money wired from family
  • Documents abuses, advocates for migrant rights

Funding: Begs donations, sells food in streets, accepts donations from parishioners (many oppose helping "foreigners")

Risks: Immigration official threatened to jail him for "smuggling"; criticized by local community

Impact:

  • Thousands of migrants receive critical aid
  • Provides moral/spiritual support to desperate people
  • Network of churches throughout Mexico creates "humanitarian corridor"
  • Challenges state narrative that migrants are criminals deserving punishment

IR Significance: Demonstrates how transnational religious networks create parallel governance structures when states fail to protect human rights

The Criminal Economy of Migration

Smuggling Industry

  • Revenue: Estimated $5-7 billion annually for US-Mexico corridor
  • Fees: $1,200 (basic service) to $10,000 (luxury, by plane)
  • Success rate: Varies widely; some smugglers reliable, others abandon/rob clients
  • Structure: Networks spanning multiple countries; specialization by function

Kidnapping Industry (Zetas)

  • Victims: Estimated 18,000 Central Americans kidnapped annually in Mexico
  • Methods: Pull migrants off trains/buses, hold for ransom
  • Ransom: $2,000-$10,000 per person; families in US pressured to pay
  • Consequences: Those without money killed; mass graves discovered

Corruption Tax

  • Police bribes: $20-$100 per migrant to avoid arrest
  • Smuggler-official collusion: Systematic payoffs enable operations
  • El Tiríndaro pays 10% of earnings to police for "protection"

The Food Throwers: Grassroots Solidarity

Context: In towns through Oaxaca and Veracruz, residents gather to throw food to migrants on passing trains

Participants:

  • Families living near tracks, often quite poor themselves
  • Bakers, seamstresses, farmers giving what they produce
  • Children participating with parents
  • 100-year-old woman forces arthritic hands to make tortilla bags

Motivations:

  • "If I have one tortilla, I give half away. I know God will bring me more."
  • Empathy: "My sons walked through desert to reach US. When I help someone here, I feel like I'm feeding my children."
  • Justice: "It's wrong for our government to send people back. We don't want to be stopped going to US, so how can we stop Central Americans?"
  • Humanity: "Can you imagine how far they've come?"

IR Significance:

  • Demonstrates cosmopolitan solidarity across national boundaries
  • Challenges state monopoly on determining who deserves assistance
  • Creates transnational community of care
  • Shows how ordinary people resist state violence through everyday acts

Debate: Smugglers—Criminals or Humanitarians?

State/law enforcement view:

  • Smugglers are criminals profiting from illegal activity
  • They endanger lives, abandon clients, sometimes work with kidnappers
  • Smuggling finances organized crime
  • Must be prosecuted to deter illegal immigration

Alternative view:

  • Smugglers provide essential service migrants need
  • Border enforcement creates demand for smuggling services
  • Many smugglers (like El Tiríndaro) are reliable, care about clients
  • Without smugglers, more migrants would die attempting journey alone

Book's evidence: El Tiríndaro helps Enrique multiple times, keeps promises, doesn't charge excessive fees. Yet he's an addict involved in crime. Moral complexity resists easy categorization.

Regional Dynamics: The Migration Corridor

The Central America-Mexico-US System

Three Interconnected Regions

Origin (Honduras/Central America):

  • Extreme poverty, limited opportunities
  • Weak state capacity, corruption endemic
  • Gang violence, highest murder rates globally
  • Post-conflict societies (civil wars of 1980s)
  • US intervention legacy (support for dictators, coups)

Transit (Mexico):

  • Caught between empathy for migrants and US pressure to enforce
  • Limited capacity to control borders, interior
  • Cartel violence spillover affects migrants
  • Corrupt law enforcement preys on migrants
  • 2011 law grants Central Americans transit rights (implementation weak)

Destination (United States):

  • Economic magnet: wages 10-50x higher
  • Structural demand for low-wage labor
  • Established immigrant communities provide networks
  • Contradictory policies: enforcement theater vs. economic reality
  • Growing anti-immigrant sentiment, state-level legislation

Honduras: The Roots of Migration

Economic indicators (2000s):

  • GDP per capita: ~$2,000 (vs. US $40,000)
  • 90% of population lives on <$1/day
  • Factory wages: $40-$120/month
  • Unemployment/underemployment: Over 50%
  • School attendance: 11% of children never attend; most don't finish 6th grade

Social conditions:

  • 30% of children under 5 stunted from malnutrition
  • Schools lack basic supplies; principals report kids too weak to stand
  • Limited access to healthcare, clean water
  • Gang recruitment: MS-13, 18th Street target vulnerable youth

Historical context:

  • US-backed military governments (1980s)
  • Contra war staging ground
  • Economic adjustment programs (1990s) increased poverty
  • Hurricane Mitch (1998) devastated infrastructure

Remittance dependency:

  • Remittances: Over 20% of GDP
  • Crucial for family survival, education
  • Creates brain drain, dependency

Case Study: Chiapas—"La Bestia" (The Beast)

Geographic context: Mexico's southernmost state, borders Guatemala

Why it's called "The Beast":

  • Most dangerous stretch of migration journey
  • Every other day a migrant loses limb to freight trains
  • Gangs control train tops, rob and assault riders
  • Bandits lie in wait at La Arrocera checkpoint
  • Police and immigration agents most corrupt

Infrastructure challenges:

  • Tracks 20+ years old, poorly maintained
  • Frequent derailments (3/month average, 17 in worst month)
  • Trains slow, making migrants more vulnerable
  • Remote areas with limited state presence

State response:

  • La Arrocera checkpoint catches 50% of migrants
  • Deportations: Bus migrants back to Guatemala border
  • Grupo Beta: Small protective force, underfunded
  • Overall: Enforcement sporadic, corrupt

Humanitarian response:

  • Shelters like Olga's treat injured migrants
  • Churches provide food, shelter
  • Human rights groups document abuses
  • Insufficient to meet scale of need

IR Significance: Demonstrates how weak state capacity in transit regions creates humanitarian crisis; state's nominal sovereignty doesn't translate to effective control or protection

Regional Cooperation Challenges

Why cooperation is difficult:

  • Power asymmetries: US dominates; can impose preferences on Mexico, Central America
  • Conflicting interests: US wants enforcement; Central America wants development aid and remittances
  • Sovereignty sensitivities: Mexico resents US dictating policy; Central American countries resent Mexico's treatment of their citizens
  • Domestic politics: Anti-immigrant sentiment limits US cooperation; Mexican citizens oppose helping Central Americans
  • Institutional weaknesses: Limited capacity to implement agreements

Existing regional frameworks:

  • Mérida Initiative (2008): US security aid to Mexico
  • Alliance for Prosperity (2014): Development program for Central America
  • Regional Conference on Migration (1996+): Annual dialogue
  • Results: Limited; failed to address root causes or protect migrants

Global Governance & Migration

The Global Migration Regime

Key International Frameworks

  • 1951 Refugee Convention: Protection for refugees fleeing persecution (doesn't cover economic migrants)
  • ICCPR (1966): Civil and political rights, including freedom of movement
  • ICESCR (1966): Economic, social, cultural rights
  • Convention on Rights of Child (1989): Special protections for minors
  • Migrant Workers Convention (1990): Comprehensive but poorly ratified (no major destination country signed)

Institutional Actors

  • UNHCR: Refugee protection (mandate doesn't extend to economic migrants)
  • IOM: Migration management (focuses on facilitation, not rights)
  • ILO: Labor migration standards
  • Human Rights Council: Special Rapporteur on Migrants
  • Regional bodies: OAS, EU frameworks

Governance Gaps Revealed in Book

No protection for unaccompanied migrant children:

  • 100,000 children annually make journey
  • US detains minors in prison-like facilities
  • Mexico deports without protection screening
  • No consistent international standards

Impunity for abuses:

  • Police, gangs, cartels attack migrants with no consequences
  • Migrants can't report crimes (fear deportation)
  • Corruption means even when reported, no prosecution
  • International human rights mechanisms too weak to enforce

Family separation:

  • CRC emphasizes family unity, child's best interest
  • Yet millions of children separated from parents by migration
  • No international framework to address transnational family rights

Debate: Why No Robust Global Migration Regime?

Realist explanation:

  • Migration is "high politics" touching sovereignty, security
  • States unwilling to cede control over borders
  • Power asymmetries: destination countries have leverage, block cooperation
  • No hegemonic power interested in creating regime

Liberal explanation:

  • Domestic politics: Anti-immigrant sentiment constrains cooperation
  • Interest group competition: Labor unions vs. business
  • Democratic publics oppose perceived loss of sovereignty
  • Institutional design problems: Existing organizations lack authority

Constructivist explanation:

  • Normative consensus lacking: Is migration a right or privilege?
  • Identity politics: Who counts as "us" vs. "them"?
  • Competing frames: Security threat vs. humanitarian crisis vs. economic opportunity
  • Need for norm entrepreneurs to shift discourse

The Global Compact on Migration (2018)

Context: First comprehensive UN framework on international migration

Key provisions:

  • Safe, orderly, regular migration pathways
  • Address root causes of migration
  • Protection of migrant rights
  • Border management respecting human rights
  • Ending child detention

Limitations:

  • Non-binding (states can ignore)
  • US, Hungary, Poland, others withdrew
  • No enforcement mechanism
  • Weak implementation provisions

Significance: Represents normative progress (migration as human rights issue) but demonstrates limits of global governance in face of state sovereignty concerns

Would a "Marshall Plan" for Central America Work?

Proposal: Massive development aid to address root causes of migration

Arguments for:

  • Addresses push factors: poverty, violence, lack of opportunity
  • Cheaper than border enforcement, detention, deportation
  • Historical precedent: Original Marshall Plan succeeded
  • Moral imperative: US intervention contributed to Central America's problems

Arguments against:

  • Governance problems: Corruption would divert aid
  • Time lag: Development takes decades; migration is immediate
  • Migration hump: Development initially increases migration (more resources to travel)
  • Political infeasibility: US public opposed to foreign aid

Book's implication: Without addressing root causes, enforcement alone cannot stop migration driven by such extreme disparities in opportunity

Development & International Political Economy

Migration and Development: The Two-Way Relationship

How Development Affects Migration

  • At low income levels: Poverty constrains migration (can't afford to move)
  • At middle income levels: Migration peaks (have resources but strong incentives to leave)
  • At high income levels: Migration declines (opportunities at home sufficient)
  • Result: "Migration hump"—development initially increases emigration

How Migration Affects Development

  • Positive effects: Remittances, brain circulation, technology transfer, diaspora investment
  • Negative effects: Brain drain, family breakdown, dependency, loss of most productive workers
  • Net effect: Debated; varies by country, context

The Economics of Remittances

Global Scale

  • Total remittances: $40+ billion annually to Latin America (2000s)
  • Exceeds: Official development assistance to region
  • Stability: More stable than FDI; increases during crises

Honduras Specifically

  • Share of GDP: Over 20% from remittances
  • Recipients: One in four Honduran families receives remittances
  • Uses: Food (50%), housing (15%), education (10%), healthcare (10%)
  • Average amount: $200-300 per month per family

Micro-Level Impact (Enrique's Family)

  • Lourdes sends $50-100/month to each child
  • Enables Belky to attend private high school, college
  • Allows family to eat, have basic necessities
  • But: Cannot replace mother's presence; creates dependency

Case Study: Lourdes's Economic Calculus

Honduras income potential:

  • Factory work: $40-120/month
  • Domestic work: $50-90/month
  • Street vending (Lourdes's work): ~$40/month
  • Monthly expenses for family of 3: ~$100 minimum (food only)
  • Conclusion: Cannot earn enough to feed children adequately or send to school

US income reality:

  • Nanny work: $600-1,200/month (live-in)
  • House cleaning: $800-1,500/month
  • Factory work: $1,200-1,800/month
  • Can send $100-200/month and still survive
  • Result: Income 10-20x higher enables family survival

But hidden costs:

  • Smuggler fee: $3,000-5,000 (years to repay)
  • Years apart from children
  • Psychological trauma for all
  • Risk of death/injury on journey
  • Exploitative working conditions in US
  • Living in fear of deportation

IPE Insight: Wage differentials so extreme that even accounting for costs and risks, migration remains rational choice for desperate families

Dependency Theory Applied

Core-Periphery Structure:

  • Core (US): Capital-rich, high-wage, advanced technology
  • Periphery (Central America): Labor-rich, low-wage, limited capital
  • Migration as exploitation: Core extracts labor from periphery without paying for its reproduction (education, health, social security in periphery)

Historical Legacy:

  • US intervention in Central America (1980s): Military aid to repressive regimes
  • Structural adjustment (1990s): Forced privatization increased poverty
  • Trade policy: CAFTA benefits US corporations, not Central American workers
  • Drug war: US demand for drugs fuels cartel violence

Contemporary Structure:

  • Migration serves US interests: Cheap labor, wage suppression
  • Remittances maintain consumption in Central America without structural change
  • Brain drain further weakens periphery
  • Circular dependency perpetuated

Counter-argument: Without migration and remittances, poverty would be worse; migrants exercise agency; not simply victims

The Gendered Political Economy of Migration

Care work crisis in the North:

  • Women in developed countries enter workforce
  • Creates demand for domestic workers, nannies, elder care
  • Local workers unwilling to do this work at prevailing wages
  • Immigrant women fill gap

Global care chains:

  • Honduran mother (Lourdes) cares for American child
  • Honduran grandmother cares for Honduran child (Enrique)
  • Care responsibilities shift downward in global hierarchy
  • Richest women outsource care to poorer women, who outsource to even poorer women

Valuation of women's work:

  • Care work devalued in both origin and destination countries
  • Immigrant women paid less than citizens for same work
  • No recognition of social reproduction costs
  • Women bear emotional costs of separation

IR/IPE Significance: Migration reveals how global capitalism relies on and reproduces gendered inequalities across borders

Norms & Identity

Constructivist Analysis: Ideas Matter

Competing Frames of Migration

Security Frame:

  • Migrants as threat to jobs, culture, safety
  • Border as frontier to be defended
  • Enforcement as necessary protection
  • Dominant in destination country politics

Humanitarian Frame:

  • Migrants as vulnerable people seeking better life
  • Journey as humanitarian crisis requiring aid
  • Protection as moral obligation
  • Dominant among NGOs, religious groups

Economic Frame:

  • Migrants as workers responding to labor demand
  • Movement as economically rational, mutually beneficial
  • Policy should facilitate orderly flows
  • Dominant among economists, some business groups

Rights Frame:

  • Migrants as rights-bearers regardless of legal status
  • Family unity, child welfare as fundamental rights
  • States bound by human rights obligations
  • Dominant in international law discourse

Case Study: Constructing "Illegality"

Social construction: "Illegal" is not natural category but socially produced

Historical variability:

  • 19th century: Open borders; no concept of "illegal immigration"
  • 1924: First numerical restrictions on immigration
  • 1965: Hart-Celler Act creates basis for modern "illegal" category
  • 1986: IRCA criminalizes employers hiring unauthorized workers

Legal vs. illegal vs. undocumented:

  • "Illegal alien": Dominant official language; implies criminality
  • "Undocumented immigrant": Preferred by advocates; emphasizes lack of papers, not criminality
  • "Unauthorized": Neutral descriptor of legal status
  • Language choice shapes perception, policy

Consequences of framing:

  • "Illegal" dehumanizes, facilitates rights violations
  • Migrants internalize stigma, hide, don't report abuse
  • Creates two-tier system: citizens vs. non-citizens
  • Yet Enrique and family are humans first, "illegal" second

IR Significance: Demonstrates how legal categories create social hierarchies; constructivist insight that ideas and norms shape material outcomes

Transnational Identity and Belonging

Traditional citizenship: Exclusive territorial belonging

Transnational reality in book:

  • Dual lives: Lourdes lives in US but identity remains Honduran; sends money, calls home, plans to return
  • Multiple locations of home: Is Enrique's home Honduras (birth), US (where mother is), both, neither?
  • Transnational families: Family members dispersed across countries; relationships maintained via phone, remittances, occasional visits
  • Ambiguous loyalties: Enrique feels Honduran but future in US; children born in US but raised with Honduran identity

Diaspora communities:

  • Honduran enclave in North Carolina provides support, familiarity
  • Spanish-language media, churches, businesses create "little Honduras"
  • Remittances and communication maintain ties to origin
  • Political organizing around issues in both countries

Challenges to Westphalian system:

  • People no longer fit neatly into territorial boxes
  • Loyalties, identities, rights claims span borders
  • Questions who has voice in governance (taxation without representation?)

Moral Circle Expansion: Who Deserves Our Concern?

Concentric circles model:

  • Self → Family → Community → Nation → Humanity
  • Most people have stronger obligations to those closer in circle
  • Question: Where should circle stop?

Nationalist position:

  • Primary obligations to co-nationals (shared history, institutions)
  • States legitimately prioritize citizens' interests
  • Helping foreigners is charitable, not obligatory
  • Can't solve world's problems; must protect own community first

Cosmopolitan position:

  • All humans have equal moral worth regardless of birthplace
  • Arbitrary borders shouldn't determine life chances
  • Structural injustice creates obligations to help
  • Common humanity should expand circle to include all

Book's evidence:

  • Food throwers in Mexico demonstrate spontaneous cosmopolitan impulse
  • Yet most people in US, Mexico hostile to migrants
  • Enrique suffers because circles haven't expanded far enough
  • Question: Can norms change? History suggests yes, but slowly
"People are losing what they value most: family and the love of their children. In many ways, these separations are devastating Latino families."

Policy Debates & Implications

Policy Options: A Spectrum

Enforcement-Heavy Approach:

  • Border wall, technology, more agents
  • Mandatory E-Verify, workplace raids
  • Expedited deportation, detention
  • State-level restrictions on services, employment
  • Goal: Deter unauthorized migration through enforcement
  • Problems: Expensive, limited effectiveness, humanitarian concerns, labor market disruptions

Enforcement + Legalization:

  • Path to legal status for existing unauthorized residents
  • Expanded legal immigration channels
  • Temporary worker programs
  • Continued border enforcement
  • Goal: Bring unauthorized into legal system while controlling future flows
  • Problems: Political opposition, "amnesty" concerns, enforcement credibility

Development-Focused Approach:

  • Address root causes in origin countries
  • Trade agreements that benefit all parties
  • Development aid conditioned on governance reforms
  • Investment in job creation, education
  • Goal: Reduce migration pressure by improving conditions at home
  • Problems: Long time horizon, governance challenges, migration hump

Regional Cooperation:

  • Coordinated enforcement, protection standards
  • Burden-sharing for refugee/migrant protection
  • Joint development programs
  • Orderly migration channels
  • Goal: Manage migration through multilateral cooperation
  • Problems: Sovereignty concerns, enforcement difficulties, free-rider problem

Central Policy Dilemma

The trilemma: Can only achieve two of three:

  1. Free movement of people
  2. Sovereign nation-states
  3. Comprehensive welfare states

Current choice: Sovereignty + welfare state → restricted movement

Alternative scenarios:

  • Open borders + sovereignty → minimal welfare state (libertarian vision)
  • Open borders + welfare state → supranational governance (EU model, limited success)

Question: Which trade-offs are acceptable? Can we escape trilemma through policy innovation?

What Works? Evidence from Research

Enforcement:

  • Border buildup has NOT significantly reduced unauthorized entries
  • Created unintended consequences: More dangerous crossings, increased deaths, reliance on smugglers, changed settlement patterns
  • Cost-benefit unclear: Spend billions, limited effectiveness

Legalization programs:

  • 1986 IRCA legalized 2.7 million; followed by continued unauthorized immigration
  • But: Those legalized experienced wage gains, better working conditions, more stable lives
  • Question: Does legalization incentivize more unauthorized immigration?

Development aid:

  • Mixed evidence on effectiveness in reducing migration
  • Short-term: May increase migration (migration hump)
  • Long-term: Successful development does reduce emigration pressure
  • Challenge: Governance, corruption, time lag

Regional integration:

  • EU free movement works within relatively similar income levels
  • Breaks down when income gaps too large (east-west migration concerns)
  • Americas: Income gaps too large for EU-style integration

Comprehensive Immigration Reform: Key Components

Path to legal status:

  • Allow unauthorized residents to "get right with the law"
  • Requirements: Pay fines, back taxes, pass background check, learn English
  • Debate: Should require return to home country? How long a wait?

Future flow management:

  • Expand legal immigration quotas to match labor demand
  • Temporary worker programs with labor protections
  • Point systems vs. family reunification priorities

Enforcement:

  • Mandatory E-Verify system
  • Continued border security
  • Interior enforcement targeting criminals

Root causes:

  • Development assistance to Central America
  • Trade policies that create jobs in origin countries
  • Address violence, corruption

Political feasibility: Repeatedly proposed (2006, 2007, 2013), repeatedly failed. Polarization makes compromise difficult.

Book's Implicit Policy Recommendations

For Origin Countries (Honduras, Central America):

  • Economic development creating living-wage jobs
  • Education investment so children can finish school
  • Anti-corruption measures, rule of law
  • Gang violence reduction
  • Family support programs to prevent migration

For Transit Countries (Mexico):

  • Protection for migrants rather than deportation
  • End police corruption and abuse
  • Humanitarian transit visas
  • Prosecute criminals preying on migrants
  • Consistent application of 2011 law granting transit rights

For Destination Countries (United States):

  • Create legal pathways for family reunification
  • Expand temporary worker programs with protections
  • End detention of children
  • Address employer demand for unauthorized workers
  • Comprehensive immigration reform with legalization
  • Investment in origin country development

For Potential Migrants:

  • Full information about journey dangers
  • Awareness of family separation impacts
  • Understanding that reunification typically takes years, not months
  • Message: "These separations almost always end badly"

Case Study: DACA as Policy Experiment

Context: Deferred Action for Childhood Arrivals (2012), created after book published but relevant to children like Enrique

Provisions:

  • Protection from deportation for those brought as children
  • Work authorization
  • Renewable every 2 years
  • Not a path to citizenship, but temporary protection

Results:

  • ~800,000 young people protected
  • Wage gains averaging 42% for participants
  • Educational attainment increased
  • Entrepreneurship rates higher
  • No evidence of increased unauthorized immigration ("magnet effect")

Challenges:

  • Temporary, precarious status
  • Doesn't help parents (family separation continues)
  • Contested legality; subject to rescission
  • Created two tiers: DACA recipients vs. later arrivals

IR/Policy Significance: Demonstrates that legalization produces positive outcomes; also shows limits of executive action without comprehensive legislation
