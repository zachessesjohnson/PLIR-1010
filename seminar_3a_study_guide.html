Understanding China's Rise, Surveillance State, and Global Impact

Overview: China's Place in International Relations

Case Study: The Surveillance State Paradox
China's technological advancement creates a dual narrative in international relations. While families like the Wu family in Hangzhou embrace smart cities and digital convenience, this same technology enables unprecedented government surveillance of Uyghurs in Xinjiang. This paradox shapes global perceptions of China's development model and raises questions about technology exports, human rights, and the future of digital authoritarianism.

The Surveillance State Model

China has built the world's most comprehensive surveillance system with hundreds of millions of cameras using AI for facial recognition, behavior analysis, and social control. This "digital authoritarianism" is now being exported globally through companies like Hikvision and Dahua.

Technology and Social Control

From social credit systems in Hangzhou to health apps during COVID-19, China demonstrates how technology can enable unprecedented state control over citizens while maintaining public acceptance through convenience and perceived security benefits.

Global Technology Competition

Chinese tech companies like TikTok, Xiaomi, and Huawei collect vast amounts of global user data, raising concerns about privacy, censorship, and potential state access. This creates new dimensions of international competition beyond traditional military and economic measures.

Domestic Legitimacy Challenges

Rising emigration from China, including dangerous border crossings, signals domestic discontent with authoritarian governance. The "Run" movement among educated Chinese citizens may weaken China's long-term power capabilities and international legitimacy.

Xinjiang as a Testing Ground

The systematic surveillance and detention of Uyghurs in Xinjiang represents the extreme end of China's surveillance capabilities, labeled as genocide by several nations and creating significant international diplomatic challenges for Beijing.

The Expansionist Question

Historical evidence suggests China's approach to power differs from Western patterns. Unlike European powers, China has not pursued overseas territorial conquest despite having naval capabilities centuries before Columbus, yet domestic repression raises questions about future international behavior.

Reading 1 Is China Expansionist? (Mahbubani)
▼

Central Argument

Assertive vs. Aggressive: China has become more assertive as a great power but not necessarily aggressive. The distinction matters - assertiveness involves using non-military means to project power, while aggression involves military action.

Historical Evidence

Chinese Restraint: Despite having the world's strongest navy in the 15th century under Admiral Zheng He, China did not colonize overseas territories. This contrasts sharply with European expansion patterns.

Military Comparison

US vs. China: Since 1989, the US has used armed forces 152 times (6.1 times per year), while China has not fought a war in 40 years. The last Chinese military action was a naval skirmish with Vietnam in 1989.

Cultural Foundations

Confucian Influence: "Good iron is not made into a nail; a good man is not made into a soldier." Chinese culture traditionally values scholars over soldiers, influencing strategic preferences for non-military solutions.

Reading 2 US-China Global Perceptions (Pew Research)
▼

Shifting Favorability

Global Trends: Across 24 countries surveyed, median 49% have favorable view of US vs. 37% for China. However, views of US have worsened while opinions of China have improved in many countries.

Regional Variations

Country Differences: Israel shows highest US favorability (83%) vs. China (33%). Some countries like Kenya and Indonesia show more balanced views between the two powers.

Economic Perceptions

Leading Economy: Half of surveyed countries now see China as the world's top economy. Greece, Italy, Germany, and Spain all shifted toward naming China over the US since 2023.

Leadership Confidence

Trump vs. Xi: Global confidence in Trump (22%) and Xi (24%) shows minimal difference, with both considerably lower than confidence in other world leaders in previous surveys.

Reading 3 Chinese Migration Crisis (NYT Daily Podcast)
▼

Migration Surge

Unprecedented Numbers: 24,000 Chinese crossed the US southern border in 2023 - more than the previous 10 years combined. This represents the most dangerous migration route, suggesting desperation among middle-class Chinese citizens.

Economic Success Turned Sour

Gao's Story: A successful small developer who built apartments for migrant workers saw his business destroyed by local government land seizure without compensation, illustrating how arbitrary state power undermines economic achievement.

Shrinking Social Space

Authoritarian Expansion: Under Xi Jinping, the space for civil society has contracted dramatically. Citizens face harassment for petitioning, social media comments, or possessing banned books, creating constant fear of arbitrary detention.

Freedom vs. Economic Security

Value Prioritization: Gao's emphasis on being able to protest freely in San Francisco, despite economic hardship, highlights how political freedoms may increasingly outweigh material considerations for educated Chinese citizens.

Reading 5 China's Surveillance State (DW Documentary)
▼

Total Surveillance Infrastructure

Shanghai's "Brain": District control centers monitor 290,000 cameras with AI algorithms detecting everything from mask violations to improper trash disposal. Authorities can track every resident's movement in real-time.

Social Credit System

Behavioral Modification: Hangzhou residents earn points for good behavior (volunteering, exercise) and lose points for violations (unpaid taxes, false COVID statements). High scores provide benefits like skipping security checks.

Technology Export and Global Implications

Digital Authoritarianism Abroad: Chinese surveillance companies like Dahua and Hikvision sell facial recognition systems globally, including technology specifically designed to identify ethnic minorities and send "Uyghur alarms" to police.

Xinjiang as Extreme Case

Genocide Through Technology: The systematic surveillance, detention, and forced sterilization of Uyghurs demonstrates how China's surveillance capabilities can enable ethnic persecution, creating international condemnation and diplomatic challenges.

Public Acceptance vs. Privacy

Convenience Trade-offs: Chinese families like the Wu family embrace smart city technology for convenience, while criticism or resistance to surveillance is virtually non-existent among the general population.

COVID-19 as Surveillance Accelerator

Health Apps as Control: Pandemic health tracking apps monitor movement and contacts, with door sensors detecting quarantine violations. The crisis provided justification for expanded data collection and movement restrictions.

Digital Authoritarianism

Use of digital technologies to surveil, repress, and manipulate domestic and foreign populations. China's model combines AI, big data, and surveillance cameras for comprehensive social control.

Social Credit System

Government program that rates citizens based on behavior, providing rewards for compliance and punishment for violations. Aims to create "trustworthy" society through technological monitoring and social pressure.

Surveillance Capitalism

Economic system where personal data is extracted and used for commercial and political purposes. Chinese tech companies collect vast amounts of user data for both profit and state surveillance.

"Uyghur Alarms"

AI facial recognition systems specifically designed to identify and alert authorities to Uyghur individuals, representing the weaponization of technology for ethnic targeting and genocide.

Assertive vs. Aggressive

Assertive behavior involves using diplomatic, economic, and other non-military means to project power. Aggressive behavior involves military force and threats of violence.

"Run" (润) Movement

Slang term for emigrating from China, derived from "润" meaning to moisten/leave. Became popular on Chinese social media as code for discussing emigration plans and strategies.

Exit vs. Voice

Hirschman's concept: citizens can respond to deteriorating conditions through "voice" (protest/reform) or "exit" (emigration). Chinese migrants represent "exit" when "voice" is suppressed.

Techno-Nationalism

Belief that technological advancement enhances national power and prestige. China's tech development is viewed as competition with the West for global technological leadership.

Data Sovereignty

Concept that nations should control data generated within their borders. China requires data localization while its companies collect data globally, creating asymmetric data flows.

Smart Cities

Urban areas using technology and data analytics to improve services and quality of life. China's smart city initiatives often prioritize surveillance and control alongside convenience.

Cultural Genocide

Systematic destruction of a group's cultural identity through forced assimilation, sterilization, and separation of children from families. Applied to China's treatment of Uyghurs in Xinjiang.

Shadow Banning

Practice of limiting content visibility without notifying users. TikTok allegedly suppresses content critical of China while promoting entertainment, shaping global information flows.

Analytical Framework: Technology and Power in International Relations

The intersection of China's technological advancement and authoritarian governance creates new dynamics in international relations:


Surveillance as Soft Power Liability: While Chinese technology offers convenience and efficiency, systematic human rights abuses in Xinjiang and domestic surveillance create reputational costs that limit China's international appeal.

Data as Strategic Resource: Chinese tech companies' global data collection creates asymmetric information advantages while raising security concerns in democratic countries, leading to tech decoupling and export controls.

Digital Authoritarianism Export: China's surveillance technology enables authoritarian allies to strengthen control while providing Beijing with influence tools, but also generates backlash from human rights advocates.

Domestic-International Feedback Loops: Internal surveillance and control systems designed to prevent domestic challenges may create international legitimacy problems that require more assertive foreign policy responses.
